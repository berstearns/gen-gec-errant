# Probing Native and Artificial Learner LLMs: How L2 Pre-Training Shapes Automatic Error Tag Counts and Distributions

## Extended Abstract

Large language models pre-trained on standard English corpora encode the distributional regularities of native text, yet it remains an open question whether models exposed instead to **second-language acquisition (SLA) learner corpora** internalise qualitatively different error profiles — and whether those differences surface as measurable shifts in the counts and distributions of automatically assigned error tags. We present an experimental pipeline that directly tests this question by comparing the **ERRANT error-tag signatures** of text generated by *native* LLMs against text generated by *artificial learner* LLMs fine-tuned on the EF-Cambridge Open Language Database (EFCAMDAT).

### Pipeline and Method

Our pipeline operates in four stages. First, we extract sentence-level prompts from EFCAMDAT essays — each prompt consisting of roughly the first half of a learner-produced sentence — preserving metadata on CEFR proficiency level and first language (L1). Second, we feed each prompt to multiple causal language models and collect 50-token continuations; the model pool includes a **native GPT-2 baseline** (124 M parameters), **GPT-2 Medium** (355 M), **GPT-2 Large** (774 M), several **Pythia checkpoints** (70 M–1 B), and a **learner-tuned GPT-2** whose weights have been updated on EFCAMDAT learner text. Third, each generated continuation is passed through a grammatical error correction (GEC) stage — using either a dedicated seq2seq model (Grammarly CoEdit-Large) or an instruction-tuned LLM (Gemma 2 2B-it) — to produce a corrected counterpart. Fourth, ERRANT aligns each original–corrected pair and emits a typed edit annotation drawn from its full taxonomy of **missing (M:), replacement (R:), and unnecessary (U:) operations** across part-of-speech and subcategory slots (e.g., `M:DET`, `R:VERB:SVA`, `R:SPELL`, `U:PREP`).

### Measures

For every model we compute:

- **Perplexity** on the held-out learner text (mean, median, standard deviation, quartiles), capturing each model's distributional fit to L2 input.
- **Total error count** and **error rate** (fraction of sentences containing at least one ERRANT edit) in the generated continuations.
- **Average errors per sentence** and the **full error-type distribution** — a vector over the 21+ ERRANT categories — enabling fine-grained comparison of which error types each model class produces, suppresses, or amplifies.
- **Combined metric** (perplexity × average errors) as an aggregate index of model–learner alignment.
- **Mann–Whitney U tests** across all pairwise model comparisons for both perplexity and error-count distributions, providing non-parametric significance testing.

### Research Questions

1. **Do learner-tuned LLMs produce a different total volume of ERRANT-tagged errors compared to native-baseline models?** We hypothesise that artificial learner models, having absorbed the distributional signal of interlanguage, will generate continuations whose error counts are *closer* to those found in genuine learner text.

2. **Does the error-type distribution shift?** Beyond aggregate counts, we ask whether specific ERRANT categories — particularly L2-characteristic types such as `M:DET` (missing determiner), `R:VERB:SVA` (subject–verb agreement), `R:VERB:TENSE` (tense errors), and `R:PREP` (preposition replacement) — are disproportionately elevated in artificial-learner output relative to the native baseline.

3. **Does model scale modulate the effect?** Comparing GPT-2 (124 M) through Pythia-1B, we test whether larger native models converge toward or diverge from the learner-tuned error profile, disentangling the contributions of capacity from those of training data.

4. **Is there a relationship between perplexity on learner text and the error distribution of generated text?** We examine whether models that are less perplexed by learner input (i.e., better distributional fits) also produce continuations whose error-tag vectors more closely resemble empirical learner error distributions.

5. **Does the choice of GEC back-end (dedicated seq2seq vs. instruction-tuned LLM) affect the observed error-tag counts?** Since ERRANT annotations depend on the corrected reference, we evaluate whether the two correction methods yield consistent cross-model contrasts or introduce systematic annotation bias.

### Expected Contributions

This work makes three contributions. First, it provides **empirical evidence on whether pre-training on learner corpora reshapes the error fingerprint** of an LLM's output, moving beyond perplexity-only evaluations to a full error-taxonomy analysis grounded in ERRANT's linguistically motivated tag set. Second, it delivers a **reproducible, end-to-end pipeline** — from EFCAMDAT preprocessing through generation, GEC, ERRANT annotation, statistical testing, and visualisation — that can be extended to additional model families, L1 strata, and CEFR-stratified fine-tuning. Third, by quantifying how artificial learner models differ from native baselines *at the level of individual error categories*, it offers a **computational bridge between language modelling and SLA error analysis**, informing downstream applications in automated writing evaluation, learner feedback generation, and pedagogically aware text generation.
